The Premise

An ancient epic. A brand-new job.

The Mahabharata figured out product management three thousand years before we gave it a job title.

A wise counselor who sees everything and is ignored — that is every PM who was never given the authority to say no. An archer who sees only the eye of the bird — that is precisely how a language model sees the world, brilliant inside its training and blind outside it. A queen who asks one direct question while a hall full of experts answers everything except what she asked — that is every roadmap meeting where the user wanted their Tuesday back and got a chatbot instead.

Each of the eighteen Parvas opens with one of these stories, told properly — then refuses to leave it as a metaphor. The story becomes a framework, the framework becomes field exercises, and the exercises become a seven-day action plan you can start Monday. Story first. Discipline always.

You need exactly twelve words of AI vocabulary to read this book, and Parva 2 hands them to you in one table. What the book actually teaches is the part no vocabulary can carry: judgment — what to build, what to refuse, what a mistake costs, and who protects the human it lands on.

  • Working PMs whose products suddenly grew a model inside them
  • Engineers & designers moving toward product ownership
  • Founders & builders deciding where AI belongs — and where it doesn't
  • Anyone who loves a great story and distrusts hype

The Shift Right — a manual for now, and for what's coming

Status chasing · ticket writing · coordination theater Customers · discovery · judgment · evaluating AI itself

One thesis runs through all eighteen chapters: as AI agents absorb the coordination work and PM and engineering roles converge, the center of gravity of product management moves toward the customer. The left side of the job is leaving — with or without your permission. Every framework in this book trains a right-side muscle, because that side is becoming the entire job.
